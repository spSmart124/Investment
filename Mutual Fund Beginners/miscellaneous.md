# Miscellaneous
## SEBI Regulations
### Role of SEBI in Mutual Fund Regulation
Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) controls
1. Mutual Funds
1. Depositories
1. Custodians
1. Registrars and Transfer Agents

The stock exchanges are regulated by SEBI. Every individual stock exchange has rules for
1. Listing
1. Trading
1. Margining

Mutual funds must fulfill the rules of exchanges with a business relationship. Anybody who is unfairly treated can appeal in **Securities Appellate Tribunal (SAT)**.

RBI controls the money market and foreign exchange market in India.
Thus Mutual fund fulfills regulations from RBI regarding
1. Money Market Investments
1. Foreign Currency Payments
1. Investments outside the country
1. Investments from individuals other than Indian residents

#### Investment Limits
SEBI has set limits for investment by schemes of different types of instruments. Refer to below table.
| Investment in | Max Limit (as percentage of NAV) |
| ------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Rated Debt instruments of a single issuer | 10% (may be extended to 12% with prior approval of Board of Trustees and Board of AMC) |
| Unrated paper of a single issuer | 10% |
| Total investment in unrated papers | 25% |
| Money market instruments | 30% (Not applicable to G-Sec, T-bills and CBLOS) |
| Total investment by a fund in a company's paid up capital carrying voting rights | 10% |
| Equity shares or related instruments of a single company | 10% (not applicable in case of index fund or sector specific scheme) |
| Open ended Schemes | 5% of net assets |
| Close ended Scheme | 10% of net assets |
| Debt schemes of mutual funds in a particular sector | 25% of the net assets (excluding investments in Bank CDs, CBLO, G-Secs, T Bills, short term  deposits of scheduled commercial banks and AAA rated securities issued by Public Financial  Institutions and Public Sector Banks) |
| Debt schemes of mutual funds in a group | 20% (excluding investments in securities issued by Public Sector) |

## AMFI
**Association of Mutual Funds in India (AMFI)** was established in the year 1955 as a non-profit organization. All AMCs registered with SEBI are AMFI members.

### Objectives
The principal objectives of AMFI is to promote interests of all stakeholders (Mutual funds and unit holders) and interact with regulators like SEBI, RBI and government regulators. Set and maintain ethical and commercial standards in the industry. 

It recommends and promotes best business practices and ensures adherence to code of conduct by it's members and other activities of mutual fund and asset management.

It increases public awareness and understanding of the concept and workings of mutual funds in the country. It also undertakes investor awareness programs and disseminates information on the mutual fund industry.

It develops cadre of well-trained distributors and implements a program of training & certification of intermediaries and others associated with the industry

### Rules for Circulation of Unauthenticated News
SEBI has issued rules to all market mediators concerning circulation of fake news through several modes of communication.

1. Market mediators registered with SEBI must put a specific internal code of conduct and controls in place. Unless the information is verified, the employees or temporary staff or voluntary workers who are employed or working in the offices of market mediators should not encourage or circulate rumours or unverified information gained from client, industry, any trade or any other sources.
2. Right to use Blogs or Chat forums or Messenger sites etc. must be restricted or allowed only under supervision.
3. Records in the form of logs pertaining to the use of Blogs/Chat forums/Messenger sites (called by any terminology) must be maintained by the respective Regulations which rules the respective intermediary.
4. Unless the concerned Intermediary’s Compliance Officer ratifies any marketrelated news through official mail/personal mail/blog or through any other mode, employees must not forward it to other sources. Violation of this rule shall be considered to have violated the several provisions contained in the SEBI Act/Rules/Regulations, etc. and shall be responsible for the action. The Compliance Officer can also be charged for a breach of duty in this regard.

### Due Diligence Process for Distributors
SEBI has instructed AMCs to put in place a due diligence procedure to control distributors for qualification.

AMCs need to put in place a due diligence procedure to control distributors who qualify any one of the below mentioned criteria as per SEBI’s directions. These are:
* a. Existence in multiple locations (More than 20 locations)
* b. Over Rs.100 crores of AUM raised across the non-institutional industry
including high net-worth individuals (HNIs)
* c. Distributor who has received commission over Rs.1 Crore p.a. across industry
* d. Distributor who has received commission over Rs. 50 Lakhs from a single mutual fund

During the empanelment process for distributors and during the period, which is a review process, mutual funds/AMCs must undertake a due diligence procedure to fulfil ‘fit and proper’ criteria that incorporate, the following conditions along with others:
* a. Business model, years of experience and proficiency in a business
* b. Record of levies, penalties, fines, customer compensations, legal suits, etc. made till date; reasons for these and as a result, the resultant corrective actions are taken and other various aspects.
* c. Review of associates and subsidiaries on the above factors.
* d. Organizational regulations to confirm that the following procedures are not
linked to sales and relationship management processes and personnel:
    * i. Risk to customer / objective of investment evaluation.
    * ii. Evaluation and defining MF scheme and its correctness to several customer risk categories.

According to this, customer relationship and transactions are categorized as follows:
* a. Advisory relationship, where a distributor offers advice during the distribution of the product, and will be subject to the principle of ‘appropriateness’ of products to that customer category. Appropriateness could be defined as, selling of a specific product which is best suited for all investors present in upper-risk categories.
* b. Execution Only transactions that are not booked as ‘advisory,’ it requires:
    * i. Written communication to the investor if the distributor has information that the transaction is not suitable for the customer with regards to product mismatch. The investor must duly acknowledge and accept this communication.
    * ii. A customer confirmation that the transaction is ‘execution only.’ However, the advice of inappropriateness from the distributor can be obtained before the execution of the transaction.
    * iii. For all ‘execution only’ transactions, the customer does not need to pay any amount apart from standard flat transaction charges to the distributor.
c. There will be no third categorization of customer relationship / transaction. When distributor groups including, group or associates distributor always make a disclosure about conflict of interest that may arise from the selling of such products to customers.

Compliance and risk management roles of the distributor must involve a review of defined management processes for:
* a. Criteria must be used in the review of products and the periodicity of the review.
* b. Factors while determining the levels of risk for the customer and the category of investment time period of such review. Other kinds of due diligence performed are:
    * i. Internal audit of transactions, exception identification, escalation and resolution process.
    * ii. Recruitment, training, certification and performance review of all personnel who are involved in this business.
    * iii. Customer onboarding and relationship management process, servicing standards, inquiry/ grievances handling mechanism.
    * iv. Any Internal / external audit processes, comments / observations made by auditors related to MF distribution business.
    * v. Findings of ongoing review from a sample survey of investors.

## Investment Restrictions
### Scheme Restrictions
The regulations set by SEBI offer limits to the investments that can be done under mutual fund schemes, and the limits thereof. In some cases, there are also collective limits for all schemes of a mutual fund. Risk mitigation in the schemes and protecting investor’s interests are the main reasons for setting such limits. The restrictions mentioned below are applicable while making the investment. Here are important few:

General Restrictions:
* The Mutual Fund can buy and sell securities based on delivery. Securities purchased are transferred in the name of the Mutual Fund because of the specific scheme.
* No bank can grant a loan against mutual funds.
* The scheme cannot invest in secured securities that belong to a group company or an associate of the sponsor. There is an investment restriction of 25% of the net assets in the listed securities of the group companies of the sponsor.
* The scheme is liable to invest in other schemes belonging to the same Mutual Fund or other Mutual Funds. This is limited up to 5 percent of the net asset value of the scheme. Fees on such investments will not be charged. This rule is not applicable to Fund of Funds.
* No more than 10 percent of a company paid up capital that bears voting rights can be owned by the Mutual Fund under all schemes.
* In the eventuality that the sponsor of a mutual fund is unavailable, its associate or group company that includes asset management company can have 10% or more than 10% of the shareholding or voting rights in the asset management or trustee company of any other mutual fund.

Restrictions related to investment in Debt Securities:
* The scheme is not permitted to invest more than 10 percent of its NAV in investment-grade debt instruments which are issued by a single issuer. Approval of trustees is must to extend this limit up to 12 percent. This limit is not applicable to Government Securities, Treasury Bills, and CBLO.
* Investment in unrated debt securities of a single issuer is limited to 10 percent of NAV and the total investments in such securities should not be more than 25 percent of the NAV of the scheme.
* Parking of funds in the form of Short-term deposits with all scheduled banks is limited to 15 percent of the net assets of the scheme. After endorsement of the trustees, it can be raised up to 20 percent. The Scheme is liable to invest in the short-term deposits of a bank that has invested in the scheme. Management fee is not chargeable for such investments by the scheme.

Group Exposure:
* Mutual Funds/AMCs must ensure that the total exposure of debt schemes of mutual funds in a specific sector does not exceed 25 percent of the net assets of the scheme.
* It may exceed the limit if additional exposure to financial services sector (upper limit of 25 percent) is not more than 10 percent of the net assets of the scheme is permitted only by way of increase in exposure to Housing Finance Companies (HFCs);
* It may further exceed the limit if additional exposure to securities issued by HFCs are AA rated and HFCs are registered with National Housing Bank (NHB) and the total investment/ exposure in HFCs is not exceeding 25 percent of the net assets of the scheme

Restrictions pertaining to investment in Equity:
* The ELSS notification needs 80 percent of the ELSS’ funds to be invested in equity and equity-linked securities.
* The Scheme cannot invest more than 10 percent of its net assets in the equity shares and equity related instruments of a company. The limit ceases to exist for investments in Index/sector/industry specific schemes.
* No more than 5 percent of the net assets of the scheme are invested in private equity shares and equity related instruments for the open-ended scheme and 10% of its NAV for the close-ended scheme

### Investment Boundaries
Every distributor and investor must know a few investment boundaries.
* Investment Objectives
* Investment Policy
* Investment Strategy

#### Investment Objectives
**Investment Objectives** of a mutual fund defines a broad investment charter of the fund.

##### Examples
Refer to below example investment objectives of few mutual fund schemes.

**Diversified Equity Scheme**: Capital appreciation from a portfolio of equity-related securities

**Diversified Debt Scheme**: Investment in broader range of debt and money market securities

**Balanced Scheme**:
* Investment in equity and equity-related investments
* Investment in debt and money market instruments

#### Investment Policy
The **Investment Policy** describes in greater details the kind of portfolio that will be maintained. Let's take an example of few investment policies.

1. Portfolio contains equity and equity related instruments between 30 to 39 companies
1. Investment primarily in mid-cap stocks
1. In excess of 50% invested in equity and equity-related securities; rest in debt and money market securities
1. Scheme may use derivatives up to 35% of net assets

#### Investment Strategy
The **Investment Strategy** of the scheme summarizes the approach that must be followed in investing the fund to attain the investment objective. This involves.
1. Investment horizon to evaluate securities
1. Method for selection of securities
1. Method of checking the correct buying price
1. Monitoring selling discipline
1. Range of flexibility to be allocated in investing in different asset classes

## Investment Methods
Various investment methods are.
1. Systematic Investment Plan (SIP)
1. Systematic Transfer Plan (STP)
1. Systematic Withdrawal Plan (SWP)
1. Dividend Transfer Plan (DTP)

### Systematic Investment Plan (SIP)
Systematic Investment Plan (SIP) is an approach where the investor invests a fixed amount in a regular interval. A certain amount is invested regularly let's say every month on a date as per investor's choice.

This approach averages cost of aquisition as more units are bought when the price is low and less no of units when the price is high.

For marginal Investors, SEBI has waived existence of PAN card for Investment less than Rs. 50000/ in a financial year. Such installments are called micro SIP.

Investing smaller amounts are easy on investor's Pocket. Saving for Goals achievable when investments are made periodically. Mutual Fund facilitates by investing through Post-Dated Cheques (PDCs), ECS or Standing Instructions (SI).

### Systematic Transfer Plan (STP)
Systematic Transfer Plan (STP) is a method of systematically transferring funds to purchase units or transferring units from one scheme to another scheme. The STP provided by Mutual Fund is a cost effective and convenient facility. It can be used by investors to make periodic investments into a volatile market such as equity or to rebalance a portfolio to book profits. Investors can choose to transfer an amount each period or transfer appreciation in a scheme.

#### Example
In case of an SIP investor's money moves from his savings account to preferred mutual fund scheme. Let's say an investor invest Rs. 1000/ each on 5th, 10th, 15th and 25th of a month which accounts for a total investment of Rs. 5000/ at the end of the month. This money remains in savings account of investor for 25 days and earns interest at 4-6% depending on the bank. If investor moves this amount Rs. 5000/ at the beginning of the month to a liquid fund and transfer Rs. 1000/ at choosen dates periodically to scheme of his choice then not only he will get benefit of SIP but earn slightly higher interests as well in the liquid fund as compared to bank FD. As the money is invested in liquid fund the risk involved is minimal and do not have any exit load. This is known as STP.

### Systematic Withdrawal Plan (SWP)
It is obvious that an investor do not want to investment their money at market peak nor redeem units at market. They can take a safer route of offering for re-purchasing a constant value of units over a period of time. This can be done using **Systematic Withdrawal Plan (SWP)**.

Mutual fund allows the investor to manage their SWPs by registering the
* Amount
* Periodicity (Generally monthly)
* Period of SWP

Few schemes even offer transferring only the appreciation or dividend.

#### Example
Say an investor aged 60 yrs invests Rs. 2000000/ at retirement. If he wants to use this money over a period of 20 yrs he can withdraw Rs. 100000/pa or Rs. 8333/month. The investor will also get return on his investment of Rs. 2000000/ depending on where the money has been invested by the mutual fund. In this example we have not considered the effect of compounding. If that is considered then he will be able to either draw some more money every month or get the same amount for longer period of time.

Within SWP an investor invests a lumpsum amount and withdraws some amount regularly over a period of time. This results in a steady income for the investor while his principal gets drawn down gradually.

#### Difference between SIP and MIP (Monthly Income Plan)
| SWP | MIP |
| --- | --- |
| It is an investment style | It is type of scheme |
| Investor's capital goes down | The capital is not touched and only the interest is paid to the investor as dividend |

### Dividend Transfer Plan (DTP)
**Dividend Transfer Plan (DTP)** enables investors to invest dividend garnered through one scheme in a mutual fund in another scheme of the same mutual fund. Investor having low risk profile gets the benefit of diversification by transferring dividends earned from Debt funds to Equity funds. Likewise dividend earned in Equity fund can be transferred to Debt funds. This is done to rebalance the portfolio and also to manage risks.

Mutual funds choose the schemes, both the ones from which the dividends can be transferred and the target schemes to which they can be transferred.

The investor must acquire units in the dividend option of a scheme to sign up for this facility.

All the units held under this scheme, plan, and option selected for DTP will be taken into consideration for transfer.

The units in the target scheme are acquired in the same folio as the source scheme.
The minimum application amount indicated for the target scheme will not be applicable to investments that are made under a DTP.

The mutual fund indicates a minimum amount of dividend that can be transferred.
The dividend is invested in the target scheme on the next business day, after the recording date for the payout of dividend in the source scheme.

Mutual funds require the DTP membership form to be registered before a specific number of days.

The DTP mandate will automatically get over when the unit holding in the source scheme becomes nil.
